Welcome to OPUS3, the 10th anniversary version of OPUS released May, 2019. OPUS3 has a new look-and-feel and new\nfunctionality. Please see the FAQ section or our blog for details.

\n

OPUS (Outer Planets Unified Search), a web-based data search tool for NASA outer planet missions, is a project of the\nRing-Moon Systems Node\nof NASA\'s Planetary Data System (PDS).\nThe PDS is a carefully-curated permanent archive of data from and supporting NASA missions,\nwhich are freely available to researchers and the general public. The\nRing-Moon Systems Node,\none of six Discipline Nodes, specializes in imaging, spectral, and\noccultation data related to the outer planets, rings, and moons of our solar\nsystem. It is hosted at\nThe SETI Institute\nin Mountain View, California.\n

\n

OPUS supports user-friendly searching on metadata normally associated with\nobservations, such as the mission, instrument, intended target, observation\ntime, and PDS3 volume name, as well as metadata specific to each supported mission and instrument.\n

\n

In addition, OPUS excels at multi-mission and multi-instrument searches by providing search parameters that are\nmore generic in nature; these are computed by the Ring-Moon Systems Node and uniquely available in OPUS. Among these are:\n

\n
    \n
  • Ring Geometry, such as radius, longitude, resolution, and phase angle,\ncalculated separately for each observation relative to the ring plane of the appropriate planet.\n
    For example,\nthis query searches for:\n
      \n
    • Images taken by Cassini\'s ISS Narrow-Angle Camera
      • \n
    • of the Keeler Gap in Saturn\'s rings (136,400-136,600 km)
      • \n
    • at high resolution (< 2 km per pixel)
      • \n
    • on the lit side of the rings (emission angle 0-75°)\n
    \n
    • \n
  • Body Surface Geometry, such as longitude, latitude, distance, resolution, and phase angle,\ncalculated separately for each observation for all bodies in the field of view.\n
    For example,\nthis query searches for:\n
      \n
    • All observations of Pluto
      • \n
    • at high phase (> 160°)
      • \n
    • and high resolution (< 5 km per pixel)
      • \n
    \n
    • \n
  • Observation Type, such as "Image", "Spectrum", or "Spectral Time Series".
    • \n
  • Measurement Quantity, such as "Reflectivity" or "Optical Depth".
    • \n
  • Image attributes, such as size in pixels, image type, and exposure duration.
    • \n
  • Wavelength attributes, such as wavelength range and resolution, which permit\nsearching for observations in a particular bandpass across instruments and\nfilters.
    • \n
